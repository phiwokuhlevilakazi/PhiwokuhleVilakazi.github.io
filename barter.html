Case Study 03 — Community · App Design

SwapCircle: Designing for Exchange Without Money

In a context where cash is scarce but skills and goods are abundant, bartering is a survival strategy. SwapCircle set out to formalise informal exchange — turning neighbourhood trades into a trusted, transparent community platform.

The Problem

Money isn't the only currency — but finding someone to trade with is hard

Informal bartering has always existed in South African communities — swapping produce, skills, childcare, and labour outside the cash economy. But it relies on knowing the right person at the right time, and on enough social trust to exchange without a paper trail.

The question this project asks: can a digital platform support that exchange without killing the human element that makes it work? Can we design for trust between strangers who have nothing but community in common?

User Research

What I needed to understand first

Before considering features, I needed to understand the informal economy that already exists — how people currently find trade partners, negotiate value, and decide who to trust.

Research Question 1

How do people currently find others to barter with — and what makes them trust a stranger enough to complete a trade?

Research Question 2

How do people determine the "value" of goods or skills when there's no price to anchor to — and how do disagreements get resolved?

Research Question 3

What would make someone feel safe completing a trade with a stranger they met online?

Research Question 4

Who is most likely to use a bartering platform regularly — and what would keep them coming back?

Key Findings

Research insights from interviews and contextual inquiry

"I swapped my neighbour's kid's school shoes for some vegetables I grew. But I'd never do that with someone I don't know — I need to know who I'm dealing with."
— Interview participant, Soweto
"There's no fair way to agree on value without arguing. If I say my haircut is worth two bags of flour and you don't agree, who decides? We need something to help with that."
— Interview participant, informal trader

Finding 1 — Trust is Social

Participants trusted trades that came through social network referrals. A stranger's rating or review wasn't enough — they wanted to see mutual connections or community affiliation.

Finding 2 — Value is Contested

Determining equivalent value between different types of goods or services was the most significant barrier to completing a trade. Participants wanted a community-informed "fair exchange" guide.

Finding 3 — Safety Concerns Are Gendered

Female participants raised safety concerns about meeting strangers for exchanges that male participants did not. The platform would need to design for this — suggested pick-up points, public spaces, photo verification.

Finding 4 — Digital Literacy Varies

Older participants were open to the concept but unfamiliar with app patterns like swiping or profile building. The onboarding needed to be exceptionally low-friction and tutorial-forward.

Design Direction

Key design decisions emerging from research

InsightDesign Response
Trust comes from shared social networks, not just reviews Show mutual community connections and community group affiliations on user profiles, not just star ratings
Value negotiation is the biggest friction point Build a "Community Value Guide" — crowd-sourced exchange rates for common goods and services to anchor negotiations
Female users have safety concerns about meeting strangers Suggest verified public meeting spots for exchanges; add a "safe swap" feature allowing a trusted contact to be notified of the exchange time and location
Older users need very low-friction onboarding Design an illustrated, voice-guided walkthrough for first-time users; allow profile setup across multiple sessions rather than requiring completion in one go
Community reputation drives engagement more than individual incentives Design a visible "community reputation" score based on completed swaps, reviews, and community participation rather than a generic 5-star rating

Key Learnings So Far

What this project is teaching me

  • 01Designing for informal economies requires humility. The systems people have built without technology are sophisticated — the role of design is to support and extend them, not replace them with something "better."
  • 02Trust is not a feature — it's an ecosystem. You can't design a single "trust signal" and call it solved. It requires layered social proof, transparency about the exchange process, and built-in safety mechanisms.
  • 03Gender and safety must be designed for proactively, not addressed after the fact. The research made clear that a neutral design is not a safe design for all users.
  • 04Community is the product. The bartering app only works if the community around it is active and healthy — which means the UX needs to nurture participation, not just facilitate transactions.
