Tribesave: Redesigning Trust in Group Savings

Stokvel savings groups are a cornerstone of South African financial culture — yet they run almost entirely on trust and paperwork. Tribesave set out to change that without eroding the human connection at the heart of collective saving.

Role

UX Research & Design

Methods

Interviews · Usability Testing · Surveys

Tools

Figma · Miro · Google Forms

Duration

6 Weeks

The Problem

When trust breaks down, so does the group

Stokvel groups in South Africa pool money collectively — but disputes, missed contributions, and a lack of shared visibility frequently cause them to collapse. The problem isn't that people don't want to save together. It's that there's no infrastructure to make transparency easy and trust visible.

The challenge was to design a digital experience that preserved the warmth and social fabric of stokvel culture while solving the transparency and accountability gaps that cause groups to fracture.

User Research

What I set out to understand

Before touching a wireframe, I needed to understand the real texture of stokvel participation — not just the pain points, but the emotional stakes. Group savings is as much about belonging as it is about money.

Research questions driving the study:

Question 1

How do stokvel members currently track contributions and group progress — and where does the process break down?

Question 2

What makes members feel confident that their money is safe and being managed fairly?

Question 3

How does visual impairment or limited digital literacy affect participation in a digital savings experience?

Question 4

What motivates continued participation in a group goal — and what causes members to disengage?


Methodology: I conducted 5 qualitative interviews (including 2 participants with visual impairment), followed by structured surveys at the end of usability testing sessions. Secondary research via fintech articles on informal savings in South Africa provided broader context.

Key Findings

What participants told me — and what they showed me

"I never really know where the money is until the end. I just have to trust the organiser."
— Interview participant, stokvel member of 3 years
"If I could see a running total for the group on my phone — like a shared progress bar — I'd feel much more motivated to contribute on time."
— Interview participant, first-time stokvel member

Three core themes emerged across interviews:

Finding 1 — Transparency Gap

Members had limited visibility into the group's real-time financial state. Most relied on a single organiser with no shared record system, creating information asymmetry and low trust.

Finding 2 — Motivation Dip

Engagement dropped significantly in the middle phase of a savings cycle when progress wasn't visible. Members who couldn't see collective progress felt their individual contribution was invisible too.

Finding 3 — Accessibility Barrier

Participants with visual impairment relied heavily on screen readers but found most fintech apps used icon-only navigation with no accessible labels — a critical design gap.

Finding 4 — Friction in Onboarding

New members found it intimidating to join a group mid-cycle with no context. A guided, step-by-step onboarding that surfaced group history reduced this anxiety significantly in testing.

Testing & Iteration

What usability testing revealed

I ran usability tests with participants across both low and high fidelity prototypes, asking them to complete key tasks: creating a savings group, inviting members, and making a contribution. The results shaped three major iteration rounds.

Finding Status Action Taken
Users responded positively to visible group progress — a shared progress bar was described as "motivating" by 4 of 5 participants Worked Kept and amplified — made the progress bar the primary home screen element
Clear contribution tracking per member increased confidence in the group's transparency Worked Retained — added individual contribution history view accessible to all members
Some users found the group creation flow had too many steps in sequence, causing drop-off before completion Didn't Work Restructured into chunked stages with a progress indicator and the ability to save and return
Navigation labels were absent on several key screens, making screen reader navigation impossible for Thabo Didn't Work Added accessible labels to all interactive elements; tested with VoiceOver after each iteration
System feedback was unclear after completing a contribution — users weren't sure the action had registered Didn't Work Added explicit confirmation screens with animation and a summary of the contribution made
Invitation flow to add group members was unfamiliar — users expected a contacts picker, not a manual entry field Improved Redesigned invite flow to pull from phone contacts with a manual entry fallback

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • 01Guided, step-by-step onboarding significantly reduces friction for first-time users in unfamiliar financial contexts — not because they can't handle complexity, but because the stakes feel high and they want to feel held by the system.
  • 02Visual progress tracking is a motivational tool, not just a UI pattern. Seeing the group's collective progress made participants feel their individual contribution was meaningful — which directly increased self-reported likelihood of on-time payment.
  • 03Designing for accessibility from the start — not as an afterthought — changed the design for the better for everyone. Clearer labels, better confirmation states, and structured navigation improved the experience for all five participants, not just those with visual impairment.
  • 04Cultural context shapes how users interpret digital trust signals. In a stokvel context, transparency doesn't just mean showing numbers — it means surfacing the human accountability behind them.
