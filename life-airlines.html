Case Study 02 — Travel · App Design

Life Airlines: Designing a Mobile-First Booking Experience

A regional airline with no mobile app was losing bookings to friction. I designed a mobile experience that guided users from search to confirmation — matching the ease of top-tier airlines on a budget carrier's constraints.

Role

Lead UX Research & Design

Methods

Interviews · Usability Testing · Observation

Tools

Figma · Miro

Duration

5 Weeks

The Problem

A broken experience was costing bookings

Life Airlines is a regional carrier that operates exclusively through a desktop website. Mobile users attempting to book through the browser encountered a pinched, unresponsive interface with unclear steps and no mobile-optimised flow — causing abandonment before checkout.

The brief: design a dedicated mobile app that makes booking feel as effortless as it does on Kulula or FlySafair — without the tech budget of a major airline.

User Research

Understanding where the web experience failed

Rather than assume the problem was just "no mobile app," I observed users attempting to complete a booking via the existing website on their phones. The friction points revealed themselves quickly — and they were specific.

Research Question 1

At what point in the booking journey do mobile users abandon — and what triggers that decision?

Research Question 2

What mental model do users bring to flight booking? What do they expect to be easy that currently isn't?

Research Question 3

How do users make package and seat decisions on mobile — what information do they need at each stage?

Research Question 4

What makes users trust a lesser-known airline's app with their payment details?

Key Findings

The real friction wasn't the site — it was the flow

"I just gave up and booked on Kulula instead. The Life Airlines site was too small on my phone and I couldn't see what I was selecting."
— Interview participant, frequent domestic traveller
"I didn't know if my seat was actually saved — there was no confirmation and I wasn't sure if I should keep going or start over."
— Usability test participant, mid-booking

Finding 1 — No Mobile Context

Users couldn't differentiate between steps in the booking flow on mobile. There was no progress indicator, leaving them unsure how far they were from completion.

Finding 2 — Trust Deficit

Participants hesitated at the payment screen. The lack of familiar security cues (trusted payment logos, visual confirmation) on a non-branded browser window created anxiety around entering card details.

Finding 3 — Package Confusion

Seat and package options were presented as a wall of text with no visual differentiation. Users couldn't quickly compare options or understand what each tier included.

Finding 4 — No Personalisation

Returning users had to re-enter all passenger details every time. No account system meant every booking felt like starting from scratch — increasing cognitive load.

User Personas

Who I was designing for

L

Lerato, 31

Sales representative · Johannesburg · Frequent domestic traveller, always books on mobile

Goals

  • Book quickly between client meetings
  • Know exactly what's included in her fare
  • Get a confirmation she can show at the airport

Frustrations

  • Unclear steps make her doubt the booking went through
  • Re-entering details every time wastes time
  • Tiny tap targets on mobile web

Behaviours

  • Books flights on her phone during commutes
  • Compares 2–3 airlines before deciding
  • Trusts apps over mobile websites
S

Sipho, 52

Small business owner · Cape Town · Infrequent flyer, low digital confidence

Goals

  • Complete a booking without calling the airline
  • Understand what he's paying for before confirming
  • Feel confident the payment was processed

Frustrations

  • Jargon in fare descriptions (flex, saver, etc.)
  • Fear of double-booking or being charged twice
  • No human to ask when confused

Behaviours

  • Reads every screen carefully before tapping
  • Prefers plain language over airline jargon
  • Books in advance, rarely same-day

Testing & Iteration

What usability testing revealed

FindingStatusAction Taken
A prominent step indicator ("Step 2 of 5") reduced confusion about how much was left in the flow Worked Retained across all booking stages with animated transitions between steps
Visual fare cards (with icon-based feature lists) helped users compare options faster than text descriptions Worked Expanded this pattern to seat selection and luggage add-ons
Users missed the "continue" button when it appeared below the fold — two participants thought the page had loaded incorrectly Didn't Work Moved primary CTA to a sticky footer bar, always visible regardless of scroll position
Payment screen lacked visible security cues — Sipho stopped and said he "wasn't sure it was safe" Didn't Work Added SSL badge, card logos, and a "your payment is encrypted" reassurance line above the submit button
Confirmation screen didn't surface the booking reference prominently — users scrolled looking for it Improved Redesigned confirmation to lead with a large booking reference, with "Save to Wallet" and "Email confirmation" as immediate CTAs

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • 01A clear, guided flow significantly improves user confidence — especially for infrequent travellers. The simpler the decision architecture, the fewer the drop-offs, even for users who are unfamiliar with digital booking.
  • 02Trust is a design problem. Users make unconscious assessments of an app's safety within the first few screens. Visual cues — logos, reassurance copy, familiar payment icons — are as important as functional security.
  • 03Sticky CTAs solve a specific mobile problem: the fold. Removing the need to scroll to find the next action eliminated a common point of confusion and drop-off that I hadn't anticipated before testing.
  • 04Observation revealed what interviews couldn't. Watching Sipho pause at the payment screen and verbalise his hesitation gave me insight that no survey question would have surfaced.
